Framework

The structure that makes building a company with your partner work. Clear roles, a decision framework, and a protocol for keeping the personal and the professional separate without losing either.

The model

Who owns what

Clear ownership is not about hierarchy, it is how two people who trust each other stop second-guessing each other and go faster.

Shared ownership

All major strategic pivots, co-founder compensation, equity decisions, hiring of senior roles, and decisions that materially change the direction of Amaea require both co-founders to agree. Neither overrules the other here.

Decision framework

Who decides what

Three tiers. Most decisions are Tier 1 or 2. Tier 3 requires consensus. When in doubt, name the tier out loud before the conversation starts.

Tier 1

Hasna decides

Commercial and domain decisions within the CEO mandate

Client pricing Sales strategy Brand direction Compliance positioning Conference attendance Partnership terms Investor narrative Marketing spend
Tier 2

Milan decides

Technical and product decisions within the CTO mandate

Tech stack choices Infrastructure provider Build vs buy API design AI model selection Release schedule Security architecture Engineering tooling
Tier 3

Both must agree

Decisions that define the company or the co-founder relationship

Equity structure Fundraising terms Major pivots Senior hires (first 10) Co-founder salaries Exit decisions Legal structure Investor selection

Boundaries

Keeping work in work

Work has a container

Amaea conversations happen in working hours or a designated session. Not at dinner, not in bed, not in the first hour of the morning. The container makes the rest of life safe.

The co-founder hat is removable

Milan is your CTO at work. He is your partner at home. If you are not sure which mode you are in, ask: "Is this a work conversation or a relationship conversation?" That one question prevents most conflicts.

Monthly founder date

One evening a month, dedicated to talking about the future you are building together. Not operational, not problem-solving. The long game. Use it to remember why you are doing this.

Annual co-founder reset

Once a year, a proper co-founder review. How are roles working, what has changed, are the decision tiers right, what would you change? Do this before resentment has a chance to build.

Rhythm

Weekly cadence

Monday

Co-founder sync (30 min)

What are we each working on this week? Any blockers that need the other person? Standing agenda, no prep required.

Thursday

Product check-in (30 min)

Progress against build targets. Anything that needs a joint decision? Focused, not a status report.

Monthly

Metrics and direction review (1 hr)

KPIs, pipeline, runway, what's working and what needs to change. Strategic layer, not tactical.

Async

Default communication

Use a dedicated work channel, not personal WhatsApp, for day-to-day Amaea updates. Keeps work searchable and out of personal space.

When you disagree

The conflict protocol

Disagreement between co-founders is normal and useful. It becomes dangerous when it has no structure. This protocol turns a conflict into a conversation.

Step 1

Name the tier

Is this Tier 1, 2, or 3? If Tier 1 or 2, the owner has the call. If Tier 3, you work through it together. Naming the tier first removes half the tension.

Step 2

24-hour rule

No major decisions in the middle of a disagreement. If it is heated, park it for 24 hours. The decision almost never needs to be made in the moment.

Step 3

State the real concern

"I am worried that..." not "You always...". What is the underlying fear or risk? Most co-founder conflicts are about something one layer deeper than the surface issue.

Step 4

Bring in a third view

If you have genuinely tried and cannot resolve it, an advisor or mentor breaks the deadlock. Not to arbitrate, but to give a perspective neither of you can see.

The foundation

You have already been tested by pressure most co-founder pairs never face, and you came out stronger. The framework exists to protect what you have built between you, not to replace trust with process. Use it when things get complicated. Trust each other the rest of the time.
